History

Along the southwest coast of what is now Alaska, the Yup’ik people call various villages on the Yukon River, known as Kwiqpak in Yup’ik, home (Ayunerak et al. 2014). As an Inuit group they have lived in Alaska for thousands of years, forming their own cultural identity with shared beliefs and practices. Many settlements, established hundreds of years ago, have become the Yup’ik villages of today.

First colonized by Russia in 1741, Alaska was sold to the United States of America in 1867 (Harvard Project 2008). The Indian Reorganization Act (IRA) of 1934 was used to create formal reserves similar to the Native American reservations of the lower 48 states, but of the six established, only one still exists today. Due to the discovery of oil and the new statehood of Alaska, the Alaska Native Claims Settlement Act (ANCSA) of 1971 created multiple for-profit corporations, the Alaska Native Corporations (ANCs) and Alaska Native Village Corporations (ANVCs), of which Alaska Natives became shareholders and were able to vote to acquire land (Harvard Project 2008). This was done so that the land is fee simple (privately owned), meaning that there is a lack of restrictions on land use, allowing for the extraction of natural resources. While this act did not affect land owned by Alaska Natives through previous allotment, land conveyed to tribes and villages through these corporations is not considered to be part of Indian Country, meaning that the land has no significant use restrictions and that tribes have limited power to exercise jurisdiction over non-tribe members on this land (Forbes 2023).

Due to ANCSA, tribal membership in Alaska is defined based on villages rather than tribal identity, unlike the rest of the USA. However, this definition by the government complicates that there are shared Indigenous identities, as can be seen with the Yup’ik. Historically they did have permanent settlements, but also traveled to various camps for seasonal harvesting. Many families would migrate, following different food sources with this seminomadic lifestyle (Ayunerak et al. 2014). Both the previous Allotment Act and ANCSA are an attempt to recognize Native land claims and respect this migration, while also settling what land is owned and can be used by the state of Alaska (Forbes 2023). Hunting, gathering and fishing are still an integral part of subsistence in contemporary Yup’ik populations, but many now live a more settled lifestyle in the established villages (Harvard Project 2008; Ayunerak et al. 2014).

Causes and Effects of Domestic Violence

For many indigenous communities, especially in rural areas such as Alaska, the issue of Domestic violence, is one that is incredibly complex and is affected by generations of trauma and structural violence against Native and Indigenous peoples. It must be noted that the normalization of domestic violence, especially against women and children, is a uniquely colonial concept, and this assists in understanding the generational trauma from boarding schools with the intent to indoctrinate indigenous children with western beliefs and behaviors would play a role. However, the circumstances of domestic violence surrounding Alaskan communities, and especially rural communities are uniquely difficult given the environment in which many of these individuals live. The cases of domestic violence, are different and vary from individual to individual, however, it is worthwhile to take into account how the Alaskan environment plays a significant role in the experiences of domestic violence survivors as well as the shelters and services that work to combat it, and help those affected heal.

In one of the rural Alaskan communities, a Women’s shelter that is run for and by indigenous women in that community plays an important role in recognizing the specific struggle that is faced by women in these rural communities. For many, the land and weather play a detrimental factor in the escape from domestic violence; for much of the Alaskan winter, the weather becomes an integral part of the distinction between existing in the environment and living in it. Given the landscape of Alaska, the ability to travel is limited. “...rurality in Alaska differs from other states in that many villages are not on a road system and are accessible only by plane, boat, snow machine, or dog sled.” (Shepard, 2001) Combined with the extreme weather conditions, the travel systems in rural Alaska hold a significant role in how domestic violence victims are unable to leave dangerous situations, and why it may take a public safety officer or a state trooper hours before they are able to assist in an unsafe situation.

This is especially important given the isolation that occurs as a result of the weather and the importance of the impact of having shelters and services with the intention of focusing on providing care that is based on indigenous practices and beliefs with the treacherous landscape.

Community Action

The Yup’ik Women’s Coalition (YWC) is a nonprofit organization that is working to end violence against women and children through culturally relevant education and programs. According to their website, this includes the strengthening of traditional Yup’ik beliefs and teachings. They serve various communities along the Yukon River, including the village of Emmonak (YWC n.d.). This is where the Emmonak Women’s Shelter is based, providing a safe place from domestic violence for women and children from the surrounding villages:

Domestic abuse has become increasingly common in Yup’ik communities, especially in connection with how prolific alcoholism has become (Andre 2013). Due to the insufficiency of law enforcement, especially in rural areas, the shelter has worked to train “victim resource advocates” who work to connect those who have experienced violence with the shelter and other resources. Often, these villages do not have roads that connect them to other communities, and many women do not want to go to law enforcement, so these advocates will take women and children into their homes in emergency situations. Unfortunately, this can create dangerous situations if the perpetrator of the violence decides to come for the family (Stremple 2023). The women’s shelter then gives families a place to stay and have a sense of community with others going through the same hardships (Andre 2013). In Yup’ik culture, community and kinship are very important, but due to assimilation efforts like the boarding schools many of these values have been lost over the generations. Yupiit (the plural of Yup’ik) means “real people of the land”, and in Yup’ik belief humans and other living beings were created by the spirit of the universe, Ellam Yua. Belief in Ellam Yua teaches that all living things need to be respected (Ayunerak et al. 2014).
